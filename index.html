Introduction: AI tools

This presentation will address the profound transformation occurring in technical communication, driven by the rapid development and adoption of Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools. While AI promises significant productivity and consistency benefits, it also poses clear threats to the role of the technical writer as it currently exists. To thrive, writers must evolve from content producers to strategic partners, designers, and curators.

AI tools threaten the current structure of the technical writing profession by automating core functions and altering market expectations.

The primary danger stems from the automation of routine writing tasks. Modern AI can generate content such as user manuals, API documentation, or release notes from structured input, and can automatically rephrase or localize content. This capability allows organizations to rely on AI for first drafts or even final content for straightforward documentation, thereby reducing the need for human writers to handle repetitive work.

Furthermore, AI is increasingly integrated directly into development pipelines (e.g., GitHub Copilot and Doxygen extensions), allowing developers to produce readable documentation output from code and thus bypassing technical writers entirely for certain documentation types. This efficiency pressure, coupled with AI‚Äôs promise of faster turnaround and lower costs, threatens teams under budget constraints, which may lead to downsizing and a shift toward keeping only a few senior writers to oversee AI outputs. Limits of AI tools

Some AI tools may require sources to be provided beforehand. However, giving them too many sources can be counterproductive. An excess of information can affect the quality of the output and lead to errors, commonly referred to as AI hallucinations. Therefore, it is important to know how to limit the number of sources provided.

The optimal use of these tools is not very intuitive. They need to be guided and fed, sometimes through multiple queries, to approach the desired result. It is important to know how to phrase things correctly and to be very precise.

The result obtained is not always optimal. When summarizing a long document, essential information may be omitted while less useful details are retained. The information they provide can also be inaccurate.

Some(visual) creations of these tools are far from perfect (text errors in Gemini, structural issues in ChatGPT).

It is not certain that the work produced fully adheres to ethical standards. ChatGPT provides images sourced from the web without attribution. Using them without caution could constitute plagiarism.

It is necessary to manually check, supplement, and correct the work produced.

It is also important to get into the habit of using two or more tools for the same task. For example, one tool can generate instructions for a poster that will then be created by another. Not everyone necessarily has these habits. As a result, some people may not be able to use these tools optimally.

Another risk associated with these AI tools, in technical writing as elsewhere, is dependency. Even if one knows how to use them optimally, there is a risk of relying on them too much and forgetting that they can make mistakes and produce errors.

Conclusion

In conclusion, we remind you that AI tools can be a tremendous asset for technical writing (researching documentation, providing guidance, producing various outputs‚Ä¶) and can save valuable time.
